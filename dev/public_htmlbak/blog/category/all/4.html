February 23, 2017 - Guest Speaker: Anthony Von Der Muhll 

2/24/2017

0 Comments

 
   Our guest speaker this week was Anthony Von Der Muhll. He is a licensed acupuncturist and Chinese medicine professor at Five Branches University in Santa Cruz. During our club meeting on Thursday, he spoke about his education, his life and life experiences, Chinese medicine and acupuncture, and more. Anthony is an alumni of UC Santa Cruz, and his father was a political science professor at UCSC years ago.
   Anthony has lived in Kenya, Uganda, and Ethiopia which helped shape him into who he is and helped him realize what he wanted to do with his life. He graduated from UCSC with a Bachelor’s degree in history which may beg the question, how did he come to be an acupuncturist? Well here’s the story. After graduating from UCSC with a degree in history, Anthony got involved in community organizing in San Francisco. Some of the projects he worked on were affordable housing and free medical clinics. He also focused on the epidemic of lead poisoning in children and trying to fix this problem. This focus, and working in the free medical clinics, is how Anthony initially got interested in healthcare in general. What got him interested in Chinese medicine specifically was a bad knee injury he got from playing soccer in his twenties. During recovery for this injury, he took up Tai Ji. Anthony claims that this helped him recover faster and much better than physical therapy and other such usual recovery methods. Tai Ji was his first step into Chinese Medicine. This also got him interested in Chinese herb shops and other Chinese medicine techniques.
    After eight to ten years of working as a community organizer, he was ready for something new. Community organizing was not a steady job and he had no insurance so he wanted to find a more stable job. Due to his knee injury and his interest in sports, he had an interest in pursuing physical therapy and sports medicine. As someone who got a degree in history, he had not taken the classes necessary to become a physical therapist so he decided to go back to school and take these classes. He took classes at City College of San Francisco and San Francisco State University.
   After taking some classes, Anthony decided to pursue Chinese medicine and he went to San Diego to complete a four-year Chinese medicine program. He finished out this program at Five Branches University in Santa Cruz, which is where he works now as an acupuncturist and teacher. Anthony received his acupuncturist license in 2003. Around the time that Anthony got licensed as an acupuncturist, Chinese medicine was a small field and it was difficult for him to find work. As Chinese medicine became more popular, work became easier to find and he now works as an acupuncturist with a focus on sports medicine.
    We asked Anthony what the most interesting part of his job is and, as most doctors would probably tell you, he says it’s the patients. The diversity of his patients and observing their attitudes/outlooks on life in relation to their ailments or injuries is his favorite part of the job. Anthony says it’s “very fascinating” seeing patients with manageable pain whose marriages are falling apart, who quit their jobs, who have depression, etc. He recalls one case of a patient who had surgery to treat a hernia. The patient’s femoral artery was nicked during surgery causing extreme amounts of blood to pool in his leg on the surgery table. Due to this, his leg lost nearly all muscle function but for some reason, Anthony recalls this patient being very cheerful and positive. Anthony called him “incredibly versatile.”
   Another patient Anthony spoke of was an elite competitive dancer and downhill skier who was extremely fit and extremely healthy. This patient came to see Anthony due to a pain he felt in his hip once during a performance over a year previous that he had never felt again. Anthony recalls this patient being rude and angry about his nonexistent hip pain. He then made the comparison between this patient and another who came in with a long list of ailments and health problems, one of which was a SUNCT headache. This is said to be the most excruciating type of headache, feeling as though there is an axe buried in your skull at all times, yet this patient was very kind and positive. It begs the question, who is the healthy one? What determines health more, quality of life or level of pain? Anthony believes the answer is quality of life.
   Along with speaking to us about his life and his profession, he taught us about Chinese Medicine, specifically acupuncture, and medicine in general.  The five main branches of medicine, as told by Anthony, are allopathic, naturopathic, homeopathic, osteopathic and chiropractic. The four main branches of Chinese medicine are acupuncture, diet/herbal/animal/mineral, tuina, and taiji/qigong/daoyin.
   The main difference between Western medicine and Chinese medicine is that Chinese medicine looks at everything and looks at the person as a whole rather than focusing on only the problem. Western medicine, on the other hand, looks at only the problem and tries to solve just that problem. Chinese medicine focuses on very subtle changes in hair quality, skin texture, muscle tone, etc. to detect imbalances and dysfunctions that could cause problems in the future.
   Chinese medicine was not always legal in the United States as it is now although Chinese medicine had actually been practiced in California longer than western medicine. Chinese herb shops and acupuncture specialists used to be underground. During the civil rights era, this was used against Asian Americans as reason for arrest. Back then, puncturing the body with needles without being a licensed doctor was illegal. The FDA started regulating these practice very harshly and Asian communities that practiced this type of medicine were raided. In 1975, Jerry Brown legalized acupuncture as a specialty that doesn’t need a Medical Doctorate to be performed. California was one of the first states to legal licensing for acupuncture, and now has an estimated 17,000 licensed acupuncturists. Fun fact: licensing for acupuncturists must be renewed every two years and required a licensing fee and fifty total hours of classes (in two years) to stay up-to-date on new medical procedure, just like any other doctor.
  Acupuncture is it’s own specialty within Chinese medicine and it has many different uses such as treating pain, fertility problems, mood disorders, neurological disorders, etc. Acupuncture provokes the body’s own tissues for self healing, and can help restore the nervous system in people who have neurological disorders  such as synesthesia. Many people wonder if acupuncture hurts or if it really works, so we asked Anthony what he thought. He said that pain and sensitivity to acupuncture is subjective and depends on the needle thickness which is different from person to person and case to case. As to whether it really works, Anthony said it all depends on the specific person, their pain tolerance, life experiences and most of all their epigenetics or gene expression (which genes are expressed/suppressed).
   We spoke with Anthony about our club and what we do (our medical outreach trips). We talked to him about our planned trip to Peru this summer and he brought up the idea of incorporating Chinese medicine into our practice and into our club. Chinese medicine is efficient, low cost and easy to learn the basics of. This would be a great thing to consider as we have to raise our own money for supplies and Chinese medicine is a much more holistic approach to healing which could come in handy when dealing with people who do not have ready access to healthcare. Anthony was our first guest speaker this year and we were very lucky to have had him. We learned a lot about Chinese medicine, and the journey to becoming a medical professional. Having guest speakers is very important for our club as one of our goals is to learn about medicine and the medical field. We were very lucky to have someone as well-rounded and experienced as Anthony to be our first guest speaker and we look forward to our guest speakers to come!
