March 9th, 2017- Guest Speaker: Gail Michaelis-Ow 

3/11/2017

0 Comments

 
Our guest speaker this week was Gail Michaelis-Ow. She is a nurse practitioner at the local Planned Parenthood in Santa Cruz. During our club meeting, she spoke about her education at UCSC and how she got to be where she is now, and she spoke about reproductive health and medicine in general. She also happened to give some really great advice to a group of pre-med students who definitely need it. Gail started off the meeting by asking all those at the meeting what career they hope to pursue in medicine. After we all went around and said what we had planned for ourselves, she laughed and told us that we all had done a lot more thinking about it than she had at our age. Gail told us that she fell into nursing with “a whole lotta luck” and not as much thinking. Back when Gail was college-aged, it was much easier and much less expensive to just fall into things rather than thinking them through.
    Gail was raised in Glendale, California and came to UCSC in 1970 for her undergraduate studies. One of the members asked her what college she was affiliated with and it happened to be Cowell. She continued by telling us that when she went to school here, UCSC was much smaller. Porter was the newest college, and back then it was known as College 5. Gail came to UCSC with the idea of becoming a philosopher. She happened to take a science class for non-science majors which made her realize how much she loved science, and she immediately changed her major to Biology. One of our members asked Gail how much she thinks your choice in major has an impact on a future in medicine. Her response was that it’s difficult to fit in all the science classes required to get into medical school when you’re majoring in something non-science related, but she also thinks a well-rounded education is extremely important. Another member asked her what the hardest class she ever took in college was, to which she replied statistics. She said she memorized how to do it, but never really actually understood it. She also told us that always loved social sciences and humanities, and all her biology classes. Chemistry freaked her out at first but it grew on her. She says it’s “kind of like learning a foreign language.” All of us at VAW couldn’t agree more.
    Around the time that Gail was finishing her undergraduate education, the Vietnam War was still going on. She told us that this was something that made her want to get involved and help in any way she could. A friend of hers was going to nursing school and told Gail that she should as well, so that’s exactly what she did. She wanted to stay in Santa Cruz so she enrolled in a nursing program at Cabrillo College. She got involved in Planned Parenthood at this time too, deciding to volunteer there. She got into volunteering at Planned Parenthood ironically enough when she went to the county clinic to get birth control. While she was there, a lady gave a rap about birth control before she was allowed to get it, and this lady was a volunteer at Planned Parenthood. Gail told us that she wanted to do that so she decided to volunteer at Planned Parenthood. Back then, Planned Parenthood was only a telephone number, not a clinic. By the end of nursing school, she had loads of volunteer experience at multiple clinics and Planned Parenthood, and she knew that she wanted to be involved in women's healthcare. The first actual Planned Parenthood clinic in Santa Cruz opened in 1976. Gail started working there and has worked there ever since. She told us that she looks forward to going to work everyday and seeing patients, and she emphasized how important it is to find this in a career.
We asked Gail if she ever sees any problem or difficult patients, and she had a great answer. First of all, she said she rarely has problem patients but she followed up by saying that she thinks part of the reason is because she comes to work with a cheerful attitude. I personally think that's important to remember for the future: patients will have a better attitude towards you if you have a better attitude towards them. Another reason she said the doesn't have many problem patients is due to the fact that they mainly deal with reproductive health so their patients are not usually super sick or unhealthy. The only people she's dealt with who have been negative or problem patients, have been patients they can't help. For example, sometimes women come in looking to get birth control but they can't get it due to high blood pressure.
From the 1970s to the mid 1990s Gail worked in reproductive health. She found out about a training program to learn how to do a colposcopy so she could do the procedure and now she does it regularly. In the mid 90s Planned Parenthood merged with the Westside Community Clinic with was a general care clinic. After this, she had to take a year long program to be qualified to perform general care in order to keep her job. She was enrolled in this program from 1995-1996 and is now qualified for primary care. Gail said that working at Planned Parenthood while also performing primary care makes the job more difficult but also more interesting.
Gail mentioned that the Planned Parenthood she works at has had a lack of actual licensed doctors for a couple years now. She thinks this is due to the fact that Planned Parenthood is a non-profit and Santa Cruz is an expensive place to live. She told us that it’s mainly nurse practitioners and physician’s assistants that work there, and they’ve all been there for a long time just as Gail herself has been. She went on to explain to us that nurse practitioners and physician’s assistant function nearly identically at the clinic she works at, and they function similar to how a general doctor would. The one difference between nurse practitioners and physician’s assistants is that nurse practitioners have been to nursing school. She also made a comparison between being a physician’s assistant vs a licensed doctor, saying that the former required less schooling and overall time, and the only disadvantage is the lower salary.
One of our member’s asked Gail what her daily routine is like at Planned Parenthood. She told us that she works in 4-5 hour shifts and she is scheduled for an appointment every 15 minutes, but many people make appointments and do not show up. There are days, however, that everyone shows up for their appointments and these days are very stressful. Another member asked her what patients she sees most often to which Gail replied that she tends to see all reproductive health cases even though she is trained in primary care. She mentioned that some of her colleagues are the opposite, seeing mainly primary care patients. She thinks the reason for this is because patients like to go back to the same person. Another member asked Gail what people usually come to her for and, unsurprisingly, she said most of her patients are women coming in looking for contraceptives or testing/treatment for STIs and STDs. Men come in for this testing/treatment as well, and to get condoms.
Of course, you can’t talk about Planned Parenthood without the issue of abortion coming up especially with the current political climate of this country. One member asked Gail about abortion and the political climate around it. This sparked a great conversation in our meeting about Planned Parenthood in relation to abortion, and the current political climate in relation to abortion and healthcare in general. Gail started off by letting us know that they do offer abortion care but this is only 3% of what they do. 97% of the time they are doing things such as pap tests, breast exams, STI/STD testing and treatment, primary care, etc, and it is estimated that 1 in 3 people have been to a Planned Parenthood. That’s a huge amount of people, and a majority of them are not there for abortion care yet Republicans in congress are trying to defund Planned Parenthood due to the measly 3% even though government funding doesn’t even go to abortion care. Their Planned Parenthood has protesters sometimes that are there to protest against abortion care, regardless of the fact that Planned Parenthood offers so many other services but Gail mentioned, that Santa Cruz is “a very pro-choice community” so even if there is protesters, there are even more volunteers and escorts to help at the clinic.
Gail gave us a little lesson on abortion care at our meeting. There are two ways to terminate a pregnancy, one of which is called a surgical abortion and the other is called a medical abortion. In a surgical abortion, the cervix is dilated and the contents of the uterus are sucked out. The more common method, medical abortion, involves a series of pills that terminate the pregnancy. This method can only be used up to ten weeks of pregnancy. In this procedure, you take an initial pill at the clinic to end the development of the pregnancy. Then you are sent home and 24-48 hours later you take other pills to soften/open the cervix to pass the pregnancy. Gail mentioned that she sees patients every day for this, adding that she usually has one slot in her schedule for a medical abortion per day. As for surgical abortions, Gail said that they usually do them on Saturdays and only have a surgical abortion clinic once a month. She told us that sometimes it’s hard to look at an ultrasound and know that pregnancy won’t result in a child but she also told us that she’s “had every advantage” in her life with support from parents, etc. so she “feels strongly” that women should be able to make “the best choice for themselves.” She went on to explain that she is very pro-choice, and she probably would not have worked at Planned Parenthood for so long if this was not the case.
Gail also gave us a mini lesson on birth control pills, likely the most popular form of birth control. Birth control pills have been around since the 60s. This fact and that fact that birth control is a voluntary drug, birth control pills are probably one of the most well-studied drugs there’s ever been. One member asked about the connection between birth control pills and breast cancer. Gail responded that there is a small subset of women have an increased risk of breast cancer, specifically breast cancer that involves tumors related to estrogen levels. The problem is, we don’t know who they are and it’s very difficult to figure this out. Gail did mention, however, that if you take birth control pills for five years or more it cuts the risk of getting ovarian cancer in half. She also told us that birth control pills have much lower dosages now than they used to and the reason there are so many different types of the pill is because the amount of hormone varies from type to type.
One member asked her what she thinks about the Obamacare repeal and she said it’s personal to her. She told us about a program exclusive to California, FamPact that will pay for any kind of birth control or reproductive health services a woman or man needs. The only downside to this program is that it only covers birth control and reproductive health. Getting back to the subject, she explained that Obamacare really expanded medical care for people who desperately need it, which happens to be millions Americans across the country. If the current political control are successful in passing their healthcare laws, millions of people will likely be thrown off healthcare insurance. “Somehow they don’t want lower income people to have health care.” Gail thinks healthcare should be a right, not a privilege. Now that’s a true statement!
After Gail had talked all about her career, her education and her areas of expertise, the final question of the meeting was if she had any advice for us, what would it be? She gave us lots of useful advice that will definitely come in handy in our futures. She had two main points of advice for us. The first, somehow volunteer or get close to a clinic so we really know what it’s like in the medical field. Many people come volunteer who think they want to get into medicine but realize they really don’t like it. She mentioned that you kind of have to like being around people if you’re going into the medical field, unless you’re going into research. She says it’s very important to spend time in the surrounding that you’re interested in before you go through all your education and realize you hate it. Her second main point of advice was that if we can take Spanish, we should because we will find it so much easier to find a job and being bilingual can open so many doors. She says it’s really helpful.
The last thing we discussed at our meeting was volunteering opportunities at Planned Parenthood. She told us that Planned Parenthood offers volunteer opportunities year round. As with most volunteering, there are training you must attend. With Planned Parenthood in particular, there is an extra confidentiality aspect so you have to go through a couple extra trainings for that. She told us that volunteers can have a lot of different responsibilities based on what they want to do and how much training they have. They can put condoms in a bag, turn over the exam rooms, be options counselors, give people pregnancy test results, tell people about different birth control options, bag medications together, etc. Gail mentioned that Trump has gotten a lot of people riled up about Planned Parenthood so there has been a huge spike in volunteers and donations. Because of this, she told us that it might be difficult to volunteer due to the large amount of volunteers they’re gotten recently but we could email her if any of us were interested in volunteering. Gail was our second guest speaker of the year and we were very glad to have her. We learned a lot about reproductive health, and we had a great discussion about health insurance, which is an extremely important topic for our club to keep up to date on. The importance of having guest speakers is that we get to hear from people with completely different experiences and areas of expertise, and we get some great advice along the way!
